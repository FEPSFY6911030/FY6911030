Exploding Together: How Binary Stars End Their Lives

Abstract

Supernovae are among the most energetic events in the universe, marking the violent deaths of stars and the recycling of stellar material. This project focuses on supernovae in binary star systems, where the interaction between two stars fundamentally changes how and when explosions occur.

Introduction

When a massive star reaches the end of its life, it can explode as a supernova, one of the largest explosions in the universe. A supernova releases huge amounts of energy and creates many of the heavy elements found in planets and living organisms. Although supernovae can happen in single stars, many massive stars form in binary systems. Binary systems behave differently from single stars because gravity and mass transfer between the stars change how they evolve. If one star explodes, the companion can be hit by ejecta travelling thousands of kilometres per second, altering its motion or even destroying the system. Studying binary systems helps researchers understand the different pathways that lead to supernovae and explains unusual stars observed today, such as runaway stars or stars with strange chemical signatures (Liu, Röpke and Han, 2023).

Background

Early stars in the universe behaved differently from modern stars. Suda et al. found that the first stars had extremely low metal content, preventing them from becoming red supergiants and allowing binary systems to stay stable for longer (Suda et al., 2021). Marietta, Burrows and Fryxell modelled how supernova ejecta strike a companion star, showing that the explosion can strip its outer layers depending on separation (Marietta, Burrows and Fryxell, 2000). Observations also reveal runaway OB-type stars that are thought to be the surviving companions of disrupted binaries (Dincel et al., 2024).

Mathematics and graphs

The physics of supernovae in binary systems involves several important equations. One of the most significant is the Chandrasekhar limit, which defines the maximum mass a white dwarf can support before collapsing. If a white dwarf accretes material and approaches this limit, electron degeneracy pressure fails and a thermonuclear explosion occurs (Chandrasekhar, 1931; Thorsett, 1996). Another idea concerns whether the binary stays bound after the explosion: if the supernova ejects more than half of the system's mass, gravity becomes too weak to keep the stars together and the companion can fly away as a runaway star. The gravitational potential energy released in a core-collapse supernova can be approximated by E = (3GM²) / 5R where G is the gravitational constant, M is the mass and R is the radius. These models help explain why some companion stars move at high speeds or show physical damage after a supernova (Marietta, Burrows and Fryxell, 2000).

Results

Studies show that companion stars in binary systems can be significantly damaged by supernova explosions. High-speed ejecta can strip their outer layers or heat them intensely (Marietta, Burrows and Fryxell, 2000). Runaway stars observed moving much faster than normal are likely survivors of disrupted supernova binaries (Dincel et al., 2024). Some stars also show unusual chemical signatures, suggesting contamination by supernova material (Suda et al., 2021).

Conclusion

Supernovae in binary systems create dramatic effects. Unlike isolated supernovae, explosions in binaries can directly impact companion stars, stripping their surfaces or altering their chemical makeup. Many systems are disrupted entirely, producing high-speed runaway stars. Simulations and observations together show that binary supernovae play a major role in shaping stellar populations and the unusual stars we observe today.
