Exploding Together: How Binary Stars End Their Lives

Abstract

Supernovae are among the most energetic events in the universe, marking the violent deaths of stars and the recycling of stellar material. This project focuses on supernovae in binary star systems, where the interaction between two stars fundamentally changes how and when explosions occur.

Introduction

When a massive star reaches the end of its life, it can explode as a supernova, one of the largest explosions in the universe. A supernova releases huge amounts of energy and creates many of the heavy elements found in planets and living organisms. Although supernovae can happen in single stars, many massive stars form in binary systems. Binary systems behave differently from single stars because gravity and mass transfer between the stars change how they evolve. If one star explodes, the companion can be hit by ejecta travelling thousands of kilometres per second, altering its motion or even destroying the system. Binary systems are also useful for understanding how stars change over time. Since the stars are so close together, astronomers can watch how they influence each other through gravity, mass transfer and rotation. These interactions reveal details that are difficult to study in single stars. Computer models show how the orbit of a binary system can shift as material moves from one star to the other, helping researchers estimate how long the interaction has been happening. This makes it easier to identify which systems are likely to become supernovae in the future and which ones may remain stable. (Liu, Röpke and Han, 2023)

Background

Early research into binary systems has shown that stars in the early universe behaved very differently from those seen today. Suda et al. found that the first stars contained extremely low amounts of heavy elements, which prevented them from expanding into large red supergiants. This allowed early binary systems to stay close and stable for longer periods of time until one eventually exploded (Suda et al., 2021). Another important contribution comes from the simulation work of Marietta, Burrows and Fryxell, who studied how supernova ejecta collide with a companion star. Their models show that the explosion can strip away part of the companion star’s surface, and the amount of material removed depends strongly on how far apart the two stars are (Marietta, Burrows and Fryxell, 2000). Observational studies support these findings. Dincel et al. looked inside several supernova remnants and identified stars moving much faster than normal. These fast moving stars are believed to be companions that survived a supernova and were thrown out of the system (Dincel et al., 2024). Binary systems are also useful for understanding how stars change over time. Since the stars are so close together, astronomers can observe how they influence each other through gravity, rotation and mass transfer. These interactions provide information that is difficult to measure in single stars. Computer models show how the orbit of a binary star can change as material flows from one star to the other, helping researchers estimate how long the interaction has been taking place. This makes it easier to identify which binary systems are likely to explode and which ones may remain stable for millions of years. Because of this, the study of binary systems is becoming more important for understanding the life cycle of stars across the universe (Liu, Röpke and Han, 2023).

Mathematics and graphs

Several important physical ideas help explain how supernovae behave in binary star systems. One of the most significant is the Chandrasekhar limit, which defines the maximum mass a white dwarf can reach before collapsing. If a white dwarf in a binary system collects enough material from its companion to approach this limit, electron degeneracy pressure can no longer support it. This causes the star to collapse and triggers a thermonuclear explosion (Chandrasekhar, 1931; Thorsett, 1996). Whether the binary system remains together after the explosion depends on how much mass is lost. If the supernova ejects more than half of the system’s total mass, the gravitational pull between the stars becomes too weak to keep them bound, and the companion star is thrown into space as a runaway star. Another useful equation is the estimate for the gravitational potential energy released during a core collapse supernova: E = (3GM²) / 5R where G is the gravitational constant, M is the mass of the star and R is its radius. Although this formula is simplified, it helps illustrate how much energy is involved in the collapse. These ideas help explain why some surviving companion stars move at high speeds or show physical signs of having been struck by supernova ejecta (Marietta, Burrows and Fryxell, 2000).

Results

Research into binary supernova systems has revealed several important outcomes. One of the clearest effects is the physical damage to the companion star. When the supernova explosion occurs, the expanding ejecta collide with the companion at extremely high speeds. This can strip away surface layers or significantly heat the outer regions of the star (Marietta, Burrows and Fryxell, 2000). Astronomers have also identified many fast moving stars known as runaway stars. These are believed to be companions that survived a supernova and were launched out of the system at high speed once the gravitational link between the stars was broken (Dincel et al., 2024). Chemical evidence also supports the idea that binary supernovae leave long lasting signatures. Some stars show unusual levels of heavy elements or depleted amounts of lighter elements such as lithium, suggesting that they were contaminated by supernova material in the past (Suda et al., 2021).

Conclusion

Supernovae in binary systems can lead to dramatic effects. Unlike single-star supernovae, the explosion can hit the companion star directly, sometimes stripping its outer layers or changing its chemical makeup. In many cases, the explosion disrupts the system entirely, sending the surviving star flying away as a runaway. These effects help astronomers trace past explosions and better understand how stars evolve. Simulations and observations both show that supernovae in binary systems heavily influence the properties of the stars we see today.
