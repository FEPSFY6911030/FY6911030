Exploding Together: How Binary Stars End Their Lives

Abstract

     Supernovae are among the most energetic events in the universe, marking the violent deaths of stars and the recycling of stellar material. This project focuses on supernovae in binary star systems, where the interaction between two stars fundamentally changes how and when explosions occur.

Introduction

     Unlike other supernova explosions which occurs when a star can no longer fuse heavier elements and the battle between gravity and nuclear fission comes to an end, supernovae in binary star systems are triggered by interactions betweeen two stars orbiting each other.
     In these systems, accretion from one star to the other can destabilise the balance of forces causing a massive explosion. This process can produce Type 1a supernovae where a white dwarf gains too much mass from it's other star.  Although a massive star that is responsible
     for a Type II supernova explosion can be in a single or a binary system, the Type Ia progenitor scenarios that are based on the thermonuclear explosion of an accreting white dwarf require a companion as a mass donor to enable the white dwarf to reach its critical mass.
     (Marietta, Burrows and Fryxell, 2000)

Background

     Supernovae are broadly divided into two main types, core collapse and thermonuclear. Core collapse supernovae (Types II, Ib, and Ic) occur when a massive star exhausts its nuclear fuel and its core collapses under gravity, triggering an explosive ejection of its outer layers.
     In contrast, Type Ia supernovae arise from the thermonuclear explosion of a white dwarf in a binary system. As the white dwarf accretes matter from its companion, it approaches the Chandrasekhar limit (Chandrasekhar, 1930) of about 1.4 solar masses. Two main scenarios exist for 
     Type Ia progenitors, the single degenerate model, where a white dwarf gains mass from a non degenerate companion, and the double degenerate model, where two white dwarfs merge (Marietta, Burrows and Fryxell, 2000). Understanding this is key as Type Ia supernovae act as standard candles 
     for measuring cosmic distances and contribute to the creation of heavy elements

Mathematics and graphs

     The physics of supernovae in binary systems can be described using several key equations. The Chandrasekhar limit defines the maximum mass a white dwarf can support before collapsing. This limit comes from the balance between electron degeneracy pressure, which supports the white dwarf,
     and the gravitational force trying to compress it. If a white dwarf in a binary system accretes enough material from its companion to approach this critical mass, the degeneracy pressure can no longer counteract gravity, triggering a thermonuclear runaway that results in a Type Ia supernova 
     (Chandrasekhar, 1931; Thorsett, 1996).

     The gravitational potential energy released during a core collapse supernova can be approximated by:
     E= (3GM^2)/5R
     Where G is the gravitational constant, M is the mass and R is the radius.
