Exploding Together: How Binary Stars End Their Lives

Abstract

Supernovae are among the most energetic events in the universe, marking the violent deaths of stars and the recycling of stellar material. This project focuses on supernovae in binary star systems, where the interaction between two stars fundamentally changes how and when explosions occur.

Introduction

When a massive star reaches the end of its life, it can explode as a supernova, one of the largest explosions known to happen in the universe. A supernova blasts energy out into space and also creates many of the heavy elements found in planets and living organisms. Supernovae can occur in single stars, but many massive stars are born in binary systems, meaning they orbit together as a pair. Binary systems behave differently from single stars. Gravity and mass transfer between the two stars can change how they evolve. For example, if a massive star explodes in a binary system, its companion star is not left untouched. A supernova explosion can eject gas and debris at thousands of kilometres per second towards the companion, changing its motion or even destroying the system entirely. Because of this, researchers have found that studying binary systems is important for understanding the different pathways that lead to supernovae. It also helps explain unusual stars observed today, such as runaway stars or stars with unusual chemical signatures, which are likely survivors of past binary supernovae (Liu, Röpke and Han, 2023).

Background

One important finding is that early stars in the universe behaved differently from modern stars. Suda et al. showed that the first stars had extremely low metal content, which prevented them from expanding into large red supergiants. Instead, they stayed relatively small, allowing binary systems to remain stable until one star eventually exploded (Suda et al., 2021). Another major contribution comes from the simulation work of Marietta, Burrows and Fryxell, who modelled how supernova ejecta interact with a companion star. Their results demonstrated that the explosion can strip away parts of the companion’s outer layers, depending on the distance between the two stars (Marietta, Burrows and Fryxell, 2000). Observational evidence supports these models. Dincel et al. examined supernova remnants in search of runaway OB-type stars—stars moving at unusually high speeds. These are thought to be the remaining companions from disrupted binary systems (Dincel et al., 2024). In addition, recent reviews highlight how different binary scenarios can lead to different brightnesses and decline rates for Type Ia supernovae (Liu, Röpke and Han, 2023).

Mathematics and graphs

The physics of supernovae in binary systems can be described using several key ideas and equations. One important concept is the Chandrasekhar limit, which defines the maximum mass a white dwarf can support before collapsing. If a white dwarf in a binary system accretes enough material from its companion star to approach this limit, electron degeneracy pressure is no longer able to support it. This triggers a thermonuclear explosion (Chandrasekhar, 1931; Thorsett, 1996). Another simple idea concerns whether a binary system stays bound after an explosion: if the supernova ejects more than half of the total mass of the system, then gravity becomes too weak to keep the stars together, and the companion can fly away as a runaway star. The gravitational potential energy released during a core-collapse supernova can be approximated by: E = (3GM²) / 5R where G is the gravitational constant, M is the mass and R is the radius. These ideas help explain why some companion stars are flung away at high speeds or show physical damage after the explosion (Marietta, Burrows and Fryxell, 2000).
